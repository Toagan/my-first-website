Paul Graham's Key Advice on Doing Great Work (Slightly Expanded)

1. Motivation

Smart, ambitious people need to do great work to avoid bitterness. Great work isn't just about happiness; it's a necessity for smart, ambitious individuals. Without it, they risk becoming bitter. Making an explicit decision to pursue great work is better than implicitly deciding not to.

2. Requirements

  • Ability
  • Interest
  • Effort
  • Luck (increased through curiosity)

Doing great work requires a combination of factors. You need ability, but also genuine interest and willingness to put in effort. Luck plays a role too, but you can increase your chances by being curious and exposing yourself to new ideas and experiences.

3. Finding What to Work On

  • Follow your curiosity
  • Work on your own projects
  • Choose excitingly ambitious projects
  • Ask questions and explore original problems

Finding the right problem is crucial. Follow your curiosity, especially towards topics that fascinate you more than others. Start your own projects, no matter how small. Choose ambitious goals that excite you, and don't be afraid to question established norms.

4. Process

  • Choose a field
  • Learn enough to reach the frontier
  • Notice gaps in knowledge
  • Explore promising ideas in those gaps

Once you've chosen a field, learn enough to understand the current state of knowledge. As you reach this frontier, you'll start noticing gaps. These gaps are where new discoveries often lie, so explore them thoroughly.

5. Techniques

  • Embrace strangeness and boldly chase outlier ideas
  • Optimize for interestingness
  • Build for real people (user empathy)
  • Work hard on ambitious projects without over-planning

Don't shy away from ideas that seem strange; they might be onto something. Focus on what's interesting rather than what's safe. When creating something, build it for real people - ideally, for yourself. Work hard, but avoid over-planning; be ready to adapt as you go.

6. Work Habits

  • Arrange for uninterrupted work time
  • Take breaks and let your mind wander
  • Aim to be the best in your field
  • Be intellectually honest and optimistic

Protect your work time from interruptions, but also take breaks to let your mind wander. Always aim to be the best; it's the only way to ensure you're even close to good. Be honest about your work, but maintain optimism to push through challenges.

7. Fostering Creativity

  • Explore diverse topics
  • Embrace confusion and uncertainty
  • Start lots of small projects and iterate
  • Take calculated risks

Creativity thrives on diverse inputs. Read widely and talk to people outside your field. Don't fear confusion; it's a sign you're pushing boundaries. Start many small projects and be willing to iterate. Take calculated risks - in an efficient market, reward is proportional to risk.

8. Learning and Growth

  • Be an active learner, overcoming school-induced passivity
  • Meet people in your field and find good colleagues
  • Protect your morale through optimism and self-care
  • Connect directly with your audience

Take an active approach to learning, rather than passively waiting for knowledge. Connect with others in your field and find colleagues who challenge you. Take care of your morale and overall well-being. Maintain a direct connection with your audience rather than relying on intermediaries.

9. Overcoming Challenges

  • Accept failures as part of the process
  • Use advantages of both youth and age
  • Balance work with personal life and health

Embrace failure as a natural part of the process and learn from it. Recognize and use the advantages you have at different life stages. Remember to balance your work with personal life and health to sustain long-term progress.
